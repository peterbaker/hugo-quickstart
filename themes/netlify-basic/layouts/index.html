What's This About?

A proposed project in Lenawee County, Michigan would use an array of photovoltaic solar collectors to generate up to 155 megawatts of clean electricity for the region, at the same time preserving farmland from irreversible development.

Solar power is one of the most cost-effective sources of electricity available today and helps reduce electricity costs for everyone.

Solar energy in Lenawee would provide benefits all around...

  • Provide clean, secure electricity
  • Stabilize energy costs
  • Promote local economic development
  • Expand the community tax base
  • Preserve farmland from irreversible development
  • Keeps farmland in production while rebuilding the soil

This website seeks to provide factual information to combat misinformation.

Fossil fuel companies that have not invested in alternatives are understandably threatened and many myths regarding energy production have emerged. This website seeks to provide factual information in lieu of misinformation.

Myth #1: We can't afford to give up the land.

Only 11% of total corn acreage goes to food production and consumption. 40% of the land in the US dedicated to growing corn goes to ethanol, with another 12% being exported. The ethanol plant in Blissfield, Michigan recently closed down and many more are scaling back production, leaving farmers with a potential collapse of that market.

The estimated land needed for a 100% solar powered America is 6,000,000 acres dedicated to solar production. Ethanol production currently accounts for 21,000,000 acres of farm land.

The biggest factor in the loss of farm acreage? Urban sprawl.

In the last 20 years, more than 11 million acres of US farmland have been converted, fragmented or paved over by development projects, according to a report by the American Farmland Trust.

Myth #2: Nuclear is the only option.

Nuclear can and should be part of the mix of energy production that gets us to a zero emission future, but as far as arguments against solar go, it doesn't make a lot of sense.

Nuclear plants are far more of an eye sore, they're nearly impossible to remove when and if they're not needed anymore, and they use quite a bit of land themselves.

Not to mention they are extremely expensive, long term storage of spent fuel is a continuing issue, and they are susceptible to physical and cyber attack..

Myth #3: It doesn't make economic sense.

The project would generate up to approximately $31.1 million in cumulative county, township and school district tax revenue over the facility’s anticipated 35-year operational life.

A Blissfield Solar project would provide a total of approximately $2.5 million over the 35-year life of the project to Blissfield Community Schools (in 2021 dollars), assuming the sinking fund levy will be extended.

The project can be fully bonded to protect the community and cover the cost of decommissioning at the end of the systems’ 35-year life, does not require access to potable water, sewer services, has no emissions or polutants, and only requires access by 3-4 vehicles at a time for maintenance.

  • Get informed about the facts of solar energy.

  • Submit Letters to the Editor.
